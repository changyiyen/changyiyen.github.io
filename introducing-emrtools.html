Introducing emrtools

One afternoon in November 2018 I chanced upon Dr. Lin, an attending pediatrician, in the hospital food court at NCKUH. I had just found some time for lunch after finishing the orders for the patients under my care (it was close to 2PM by then), and I was slightly anxious to get back to the ward, as I haven't yet finished recording the day's notes. He greeted me warmly, said that he had known of my interest in medical informatics, and talked a bit about some work he was doing with the hospital's electronic medical records (EMR) system, while I wolfed down my boxed lunch. He ended by treating me to tea and inviting me to his office to have a look at his projects. It was application season for new residents, and he attempted to persuade me to join the pediatric department here. After 2 weeks of pondering, I ultimately chose a career in internal medicine, because I can't handle young kids well.

Several months later, I accepted a position as an internal medicine resident at NTUH, after a gruelling interview and written exam. In March 2019, I messaged Dr. Lin to thank him for his kindness and his recognition of my hobby. I also sent him a copy of a tool for downloading measurement data and radiology reports from the hospital's laboratory information system, since the code was written specifically for this hospital and would be likely useless elsewhere; I had intended to bequeath the code to him. That's when he mentioned that he's working on something else, and would still like me to contribute if I'm interested.

So here we are. I wrote some (admittedly poor-quality) code for working with data from the hospital's EMR system, as well as the ICU records system. It's called emrtools, and is hosted at my GitHub repository. Do note that these tools are not comprehensive by any means, and are provided as-is to anybody interested in using them. I have chosen to place these tools under an extremely liberal license, one that I wrote, called the coffeeware license, which is modelled after Poul Henning-Kamp's beerware license. I'll still be developing these tools after I leave, but do expect a slowdown in commits.
